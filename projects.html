Biomimicry of Fish Locomotion

Ongoing undergraduate research where I work on understanding and improving the propulsive performance of flapping foils using morphing. I developed ALE-based 2D models to study morphing–pitching–heaving motion and showed that morphing can increase thrust by up to 31% and efficiency by 12%. I also extended this work to a tandem-foil setup to replicate fish schools and analyzed the wake using DMD.

Ornithopter

I led the design of a dual-crank flapping mechanism to replicate controlled bird-like motion. Later, I integrated trailing-edge morphing to improve thrust and stability, and we achieved a stable 7-second flight. This project is where my interest in biomimicry and unsteady aerodynamics really began.

Trailing-Edge Morphing for Enhanced Propulsive Performance

This work focuses on understanding how chordwise trailing-edge morphing affects thrust, lift, and power. The study was presented at FMFP ’24 and later submitted to Physics of Fluids. The main idea is that active flexibility can help flapping foils adapt to their flow environment and consistently generate higher thrust.

Suppression of Vortex-Induced Vibrations (VIV)

As part of this project, I helped set up a low-speed wind tunnel facility and performed validation tests on airfoils and cylinders (up to 96% accuracy). I also developed an OpenFOAM solver to simulate the VIV response of a 2D cylinder and studied passive techniques to suppress the vibrations.

For my thesis at IIT Bombay, I analyze how Knudsen-number-dependent effects influence beam directionality and flux distribution. The goal is to improve the collimation of ytterbium beams, which has applications in precision biomedical imaging and diagnostics.

Morphing Foils — Biomimicry of Fish Locomotion

Summary — The goal of this project was to analyse the effects of trailing-edge morphing foils by adding it to flapping foils. In layman's terms, adding flexibility to an airfoil that rotates about its leading edge (front) and moves vertically. By analysing different configurations of morphing, I found out that this layer of flexibility can improve thrust by around 35% and efficiency by 12%. To increase this even further, I realized that analyzing these foils in fish schools might be very beneficial. So, a simplified tandem model was analysed, and it turns out we could increase the efficiency by 525! compared to our original flapping foil (although it isn't an apples-to-apples comparison)

Motivation

I knew that there were very few successful replications of fish locomotion. Even the best ones, didn't nearly come close to actually replicating fish motion. My goal was basically to start off with a simpler problem (which is how all great problems are solved), and try to get to the ideal undulatory motion (the one used by fish). This is also why the current project is ongoing. With morphing, we figured out that the thrust and efficiency can come close to that of undulatory foils but it still isn't good enough (ended where I started from)

Key findings

Flexibility actually improves thrust and efficiency! In fact, by a decent enough margin that it can be a viable solution for UAVs in the future (although it is not quite there yet). I found that when morphing can increase thrust by around 35% and efficiency by 12%. Additionally, when it interacts with other vortices, the morphing foil can increase its own thrust and efficiency by a substantial margin. Interestingly, the trends for thrust, power, and efficiency end up remaining similar.

What I learned

The scientific method! It was a great learning experience for me and served as a pivotal moment in my career as a researcher. After being stuck for days trying to figure out how morphing affects the flow physics, I finally realized that it affects the flow around it in a similar manner, irrespective of the flow conditions. I am not sure if I can give out more info than this since the journal manuscript is only been submitted.

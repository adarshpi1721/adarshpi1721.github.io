Conduction of Graphene and Flow Around Drone Rotors

Summary — My first research internship, where I got hands-on experience with industry-level software. Primarily, my work focused on simulating various properties of graphene nanoribbons, like phonon dispersion plots and optimized geometry configurations. I used the software ASAP to simulate these nanoribbons, and for simulating the flow around drone rotors, I used Particleworks, which is a meshless software.

What I learnt

Approaching the topic of quantum physics was quite new to me. Unlike fluid mechanics, my background in mechanical engineering meant I didn't necessarily have all the resources to fully comprehend wave functions and DFT (density functional theory). This meant I spent the initial days of my time learning about the basics of DFT, how it is actually implemented in the software, and what results we get out of it. I had to perform hand calculations, as was advised by my mentor, which meant I had to understand some aspects of the software at a fundamental level. For simulation of drone rotors, I used Particleworks, which was relatively easier to grasp due to my growing interest in CFD at the time and background with simulating morphing motion.

Key observations

The rotor simulations suggested small geometry changes that reduce peak tip velocities and potentially lower noise. As for the graphene nanoribbons, my goal was to analyze how changing the edge particles would affect the geometry. Due to the internship being only 2 months long, I was not fully able to analyze the effects of different atoms. Although I was able to analyze the isolated properties of zigzag graphene nanoribbon and validate it with previous research conducted.

What I learned

Being my first internship experience, I got to learn what it means to be in a team environment at a corporate level and how to coordinate with other members of the team. I also got some insight into how the company chooses projects and takes on clients from the goverment. Mainly, it sparked a newfound love for quantum physics (something I had buried deep in high school) and showed me how it is more relevant than I could have ever imagined.
